Last architecture chat was a fairly small turnout, and as such we ended up having a fairly focused conversation mostly around NHibernate, here's what we talked about on the day:

  • The rise of interest in NHibernate</a> recently.</li>
  • The NHibernate 3rd party tooling market and side projects.</li>
    • NHibernate Profiler</a>.</li>
    • NHibernate Search, NHibernate Burrow etc. in NHibernate Contrib</a>.</li>
    • Fluent NHibernate</a>.</li>
    • ActiveWriter</a>.</li>
    • Linq for NHibernate</a> and just how complete it is...</li>
    • NHibernate Query Generator</a>.</li>
      </ul>
      </li>

    • What tooling / projects for NHibernate could increase developer velocity.</li>
    • NHibernate discussion around a number of points:</li>
      • Single table / class table inheritance.</li>
      • Can you mix both the types of inheritance together.</li>
      • Can a discriminator cross multiple columns.</li>
      • Discriminator mapping over a range i.e. CreatedDate < 2003, instantiate a GoldCustomer, otherwise if CreatedDate >= 2003 instantiate a premium custom (change in customer structuring post 2003).</li>
      • Ability to support stored procedures in NHibernate where parameters are not in the expected order, or if multiple stored proc calls are required to persist an entity and possible work-arounds.</li>
      • Custom column types, limitations etc.</li>
        </ul>
        </li>

      • General ORM questions</li>
        • Does the ORM with the best tooling with out long-term for .Net?</li>
          </ul>
          </li>

        • Obfuscation and License Products.</li>
          • Why is it such a problematic space...</li>
          • The issues people have.</li>
          • The pain of rolling your own.</li>
          • References to the post from Ayende on his problems with XHEO</a>.</li>
            </ul>
            </li>

          • Refund policies of software product companies (especially components/libraries for developers).</li>
            • Why companies get stubborn about refunds.</li>
            • Just how many refund requests companies get (consensus - not that many).</li>
            • The associated cost of bad service with considering reactions being represented in public (blogs, twitter etc.).</li>
            • The point at which most companies should offer a refund, and should they offer it before the customer asks ex. if they can not resolve their issues in a timely manor (or at all) before eroding the money earned from the sale in the first place.</li>
